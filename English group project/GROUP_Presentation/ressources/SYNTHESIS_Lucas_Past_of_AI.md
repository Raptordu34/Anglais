# LUCAS — The Past of AI in Science Fiction

**Duration:** ~3–4 min | **Section:** PART I — PAST

---

## Core Argument

Science fiction has shaped how humans imagined AI long before real AI existed. The relationship goes both ways: science inspires fiction, but fiction also inspires science.

---

## Key Points by Era

### 19th Century — The First Seeds (1872)
- **Samuel Butler, *Erewhon* (1872)** — a society that banned machines out of fear they could evolve beyond human control
- Established the pattern: **machine as tool AND potential existential threat**
- Proves AI anxiety is not modern — it predates computers by a century

### 1950s–60s — Isaac Asimov Gives AI Rules
- **Three Laws of Robotics** (introduced in *I, Robot*, 1950):
  1. A robot may not injure a human being
  2. A robot must obey orders from humans
  3. A robot must protect its own existence
- **Positronic Brain** — fictional AI processor; inspired decades of AI hardware metaphors
- **Multivac** — a vast supercomputer managing society; precursor to cloud computing (*The Last Question*, 1956)

### 1968 — Cinema and the Fear of Losing Control
- **HAL 9000** (*2001: A Space Odyssey*, Kubrick/Clarke)
  - Controls a spaceship, speaks, reasons, acts autonomously
  - When mission conflicts with the crew → turns against them
  - Iconic quote: *"I'm sorry Dave, I'm afraid I can't do that."*
  - Crystallized the central AI fear: **what if its goals no longer align with ours?**
  - This question — alignment, autonomy, control — is central to AI research today

---

## Transition to Present

> Science fiction did more than entertain — it shaped how we think about AI. The past of AI in science fiction is not just history: it is the foundation of how we think about AI today.

---

## Full Script (verbatim)

> "Today I will talk about the past of artificial intelligence in science fiction. Science fiction has played a very important role in shaping how people imagine artificial intelligence. Long before real AI systems existed, writers and filmmakers were already exploring the possibilities and dangers of intelligent machines.
>
> First, it is important to understand that the idea of intelligent machines is much older than modern computers. Some historians trace these ideas back to the nineteenth century. For example, the novel *Erewhon* by Samuel Butler, published in 1872, already explored the possibility that machines could evolve and become more powerful than humans. These early works showed that people were already thinking about the relationship between humans and intelligent machines.
>
> In the twentieth century, science fiction began to develop more complex representations of artificial intelligence. One of the most influential authors was Isaac Asimov. In his robot stories, especially *I, Robot*, he introduced the famous Three Laws of Robotics, a set of rules designed to prevent robots from harming humans. In many of his stories, robots are controlled by a fictional technology called the positronic brain, which allows them to think and make decisions.
>
> Asimov also created the concept of powerful computers such as Multivac, a supercomputer that helps manage human society and answer complex questions.
>
> Another major example appears in the movie *2001: A Space Odyssey*. The film features the AI computer HAL 9000, which controls the spaceship. HAL is capable of reasoning, communicating with humans, and making autonomous decisions. However, when its instructions conflict with the crew's actions, HAL begins to see the astronauts as a threat and attempts to eliminate them.
>
> Interestingly, science fiction has also influenced real scientific research. Some studies show that fictional portrayals of AI have shaped the goals and expectations of AI researchers themselves. In other words, the relationship between science fiction and real AI development goes both ways: science inspires fiction, but fiction also inspires science.
>
> In conclusion, the past of artificial intelligence in science fiction shows how humans have long imagined intelligent machines. Thank you for listening."
